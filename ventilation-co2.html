Indoor air quality

Ventilation & CO2

Ventilation design connects comfort, air quality, noise, heat recovery and controls. CO2 can be useful, but it must be interpreted as one indicator within the larger ventilation strategy.

Why ventilation is not only about fresh air

Ventilation also affects humidity, odors, pressure balance, heat losses, acoustic comfort and how occupants experience a space over time.

CO2 as an indicator of ventilation quality

CO2 measurements can reveal occupancy-related ventilation demand. They should be combined with knowledge of sensor calibration, room use, airflow paths and system response time.

Airflow, noise and duct design

A ventilation system that moves the correct air volume can still disappoint users if duct velocity, grille selection or fan control creates avoidable noise.

Heat recovery ventilation

Heat recovery can reduce energy loss while keeping stable air renewal. Design decisions should consider frost protection, filters, bypass operation and maintenance access.

Sensor placement and control logic

Sensors should represent the occupied zone and the control goal. Poor placement can make a technically good controller react to the wrong signal.
